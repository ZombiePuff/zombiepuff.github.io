Purpose:

This website’s purpose is primarily to function as a study aid for help outside of class for first year high school chemistry students. It will focus on trends of the periodic table, a topic which is often difficult for students to comprehend without having much experience with chemistry. Understanding what different elements would physically “look like” can help one understand an element's electronegativity, its ionization energy, and its electron affinity. Knowing those characteristics can help one understand why different elements interact the way they do. This is one of the most difficult ideas to understand when it comes to chemistry as well as one of the most important. This leads to many students not every really grasping the subject and feeling lost throughout the rest of the studies in chemistry.

Target Audience Analysis:

The site’s primary target audience is first year high school chemistry students. This means they are mostly 15 and 16 year olds but might be between 14 and 18 years old. It will be an even split between genders, and as chemistry is usually not a required course, visitors to the site likely have at least a little exposure to at least high school level science material. High school students often have lots of activities and classes vying for their time, and typically are not likely to voluntarily read long pages of text. Most current high school students have grown up using the internet at least in a school setting and have a decent grasp on how to navigate a web page. They are however less likely to do so by using a traditional desktop computer and are more likely to try to view the page using a tablet of possible a cell phone.
The site also has the secondary audience being high school chemistry teachers. Their profile is much broader. As such so is their experience with the internet and computers. They could very likely be between the ages of 22 and 65 years old. As such it is best to design the website keeping in mind that some users may be unfamiliar with many internet norms and standards.

I asked some Chemistry teachers:

What is one of the subjects your students have the most difficulty with?
The most common answers were trends in the periodic table, balancing reactions, converting units, and using significant figures.
Which students need the most help?
Their responses focused on students that need things broken down step by step, students who can’t pay attention, and students who don’t care.

I asked some students that had taken high school chemistry last school year:

What are some of the topics that you needed extra help with in Chemistry?
Common answers were predicting reactions, the math stuff, the periodic table, quantum numbers, and significant figures.
What would make a website explaining topics in chemistry the most helpful?
I received answers like “don’t use big words,” make it simple, use pictures, “don’t make it boring,”
How would you most likely view a website to help you study chemistry?
All of the students asked said their cellphones, a few mentioned they might use a chromebook.

Content:

Based on the responses from the students and teachers I talked with as well as my own experience teaching chemistry, I have decided to focus on explaining quantum numbers and how they relate to the periodic table as well as some of the trends in the periodic table that are caused by an atom’s electron configuration (or its quantum numbers).

Organization of Information:

By explaining quantum numbers with the periodic table in mind students can get a better grasp of what makes the elements act the way they do, and why the periodic table is organized the way it is. They can then see how the periodic table can help us to understand elements better by seeing the trends that form within the periodic table.
